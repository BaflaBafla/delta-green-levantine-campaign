# DRAFT — The Levantine Engine

---

## 1-on-1 Origin Sessions

Each player runs a solo session before the main campaign. These establish the character's first contact with the Unnatural and end with Mrs. Grossman's recruitment.

All four can also be run as flashbacks during the main campaign if scheduling prevents solo sessions.

---

### Character 1: "The Voice in the Static" (Tactical Lead)

**Setting**: Gaza border, 2023. A night tunnel reconnaissance operation. Character 1 is a young IDF soldier in a Yahalom (combat engineering) unit.

**Scene 1 — The Descent**
Read or paraphrase:

> *"2200 hours. The squad leader gives the signal. Seven of you, full kit, descending into a shaft east of Khan Yunis. Intel says the tunnel is new — Hamas engineering corps, maybe a month old. Your job: map it, mark it, get out. The air is wet. It shouldn't be wet — you're two klicks from the coast. But the walls are damp, and the smell is... ancient. Like a harbor at low tide. Like something dead and something alive at the same time."*

The tunnel is wrong. It's too straight, too smooth. The walls bear carvings that look Philistine — fish scales, waves, a five-pointed crown. Character 1's radio begins to emit a low hum that isn't speech but feels like *language*.

**Scene 2 — The Split**
The squad reaches a fork. The tunnel branches into three identical passages. The squad leader calls for a vote. Before anyone speaks, Character 1 hears a voice in their earpiece:

> *"Not the left. Not the right. The center is waiting."*

The voice is calm, genderless, ancient. If Character 1 follows it, the squad leader scoffs but follows. If they don't, the squad splits — and those who take the wrong branches die.

**Scene 3 — The Choice**
The correct passage leads to a chamber. In the center: a stone altar, covered in salt crystals. On the altar: a brass fish pendant, identical to the one Mariam Abu Rashed wears in the main campaign. The voice says:

> *"Take it. You'll need it when the deep calls you back."*

If Character 1 takes the pendant, they feel a cold pressure in their chest — Dagon's mark. If they refuse, the chamber begins to flood with seawater from nowhere, and they must run.

**Scene 4 — The Aftermath**
Character 1 emerges alone. The other six are dead — three from a collapse, three from unexplained drowning (lungs full of salt water, a kilometer from the sea). They are debriefed by a civilian woman: **Yael Talmor**, who asks very specific questions about "what they heard on the radio." Character 1 never learns what happened. The pendant (if taken) stays in their possession, a strange weight they can't throw away.

**Recruitment**: Months later, a woman in her 60s — Mrs. Grossman — contacts Character 1 through a dead drop. The message: *"I know about the voice in the static. I know about the pendant. There are others like you. We need to talk."*

**Handler Notes**:
- The voice was Dagon. Character 1 was chosen as an anchor — someone who can survive contact with the deep.
- If they took the pendant, it's a Pact artifact (see PROJECT_STATE §9.3). It lets them sense underground water sources and unnatural presences. It also slowly acclimates them to Dagon's influence.
- If they refused it, the water that flooded the chamber left a mark — a patch of skin on their chest that's always cold, slightly scaled. They hide it.

### Character 2: "The Rotting Patient" (Medic)

**Setting**: Al-Shifa Hospital, Gaza City, 2024. Character 2 is volunteering with an international medical NGO.

**Scene 1 — The Triage**
Read or paraphrase:

> *"The emergency room is chaos. You've been on shift for 14 hours. The next patient is a young man — maybe 19 — brought in by his mother. No visible wounds. No shrapnel. No burns. But he's dying. His skin is grey, his eyes are clouded, and he smells like the sea after a storm. His mother says he came home from work in the tunnels three days ago, complaining of thirst. He hasn't stopped drinking water since. He's consumed 20 liters today. He's still dehydrated."*

The patient's vital signs are impossible — his heart rate is 40 BPM, his blood pressure is 70/40, but he's conscious and lucid. He stares at Character 2 with an expression of terrible recognition.

**Scene 2 — The Surgery**
Character 2 scrubs in. They open the patient's abdomen. Inside:
- No blood. The cavity is filled with clear, cold salt water.
- The organs are present but shrinking — as if the body is hollowing out from within.
- Tucked against the spine: a mass of seaweed and small fish bones, arranged in a precise geometric pattern — a hexagram with a fish at its center.

**Skill check**: A Medicine or Occult roll recognizes the pattern as an ancient Philistine binding symbol — meant to contain or channel a spirit.

**Scene 3 — The Message**
As Character 2 reaches for the mass, the patient's eyes snap open. He speaks in perfect, unaccented English:

> *"She says thank you for coming. She's been waiting for a doctor who would look. The tunnels are her veins. The grief is her blood. She wants you to understand before you judge."*

The patient dies. The mass in his chest dissolves into brine. The smell of salt water fades. The body is now a normal, empty corpse.

**Scene 4 — The Aftermath**
Character 2 reports the incident to the NGO medical director. The director dismisses it — "combat stress, dehydration psychosis, mass hysteria." But the records from that night show something strange: the patient was never admitted. No intake form. No bed assignment. The mother Character 2 spoke to? No hospital security camera captured her face.

**Recruitment**: Weeks later, Character 2 receives a sealed envelope at their clinic. Inside: a single brass fish pendant and a handwritten note: *"She wants to meet you properly. I can arrange it. — M. Grossman"*

**Handler Notes**:
- The entity was Dagon, speaking through the patient. She chose Character 2 because a doctor is the one person who can see the cost of the Pact without flinching.
- The hexagram pattern with the fish is a "binding mark of Dagon" — the same symbol appears on the War Scroll in the climax.
- Character 2's medical knowledge is key to understanding the egregore's influence as a *physiological* phenomenon, not just a psychic one.

### Character 3: "The Proof of God" (Intelligence)

**Setting**: Shin Bet SIGINT station, Tel Aviv, 2025. Character 3 is a signals analyst on the night shift.

**Scene 1 — The Signal**
Read or paraphrase:

> *"0230 hours. You're monitoring a dead frequency — 147.7 MHz, a band that hasn't carried civilian traffic since the 1990s. It's been silent for years. Tonight, it's not. The signal is pure — no encryption, no modulation, just a voice reciting in ancient Aramaic. The same phrase, looped. Your decryption suite auto-translates: 'I am that I am that I am that I am.' Over and over. But that's not the language layer — that's the audio layer. The data layer underneath is mathematical."*

Character 3 runs the math. It's a recursive proof — a self-referential loop that demonstrates the logical necessity of a universal observer. It proves, mathematically, that something must be conscious of the universe for the universe to exist. It proves God exists. Or it proves something that *wants to be called* God.

**Scene 2 — The Coordinates**
Buried in the proof's third recursion: a set of coordinates. Latitude and longitude for a location in Jerusalem. Character 3 checks the map — it's the Israel Museum. A specific sub-basement room under the Shrine of the Book.

Character 3 has no authority to investigate. They can:
- Go alone (unauthorized, risky)
- Report it to their superior (who may or may not be connected to Project Gibeon)
- Discreetly ask a contact at the museum (Character 4, if the campaign is linking them already)

**Scene 3 — The Wall**
If Character 3 investigates, they find the sub-basement: a disused storage room filled with broken display cases. On the far wall, in Aramaic script that seems to be *growing out of the concrete*:

> *"The watchers watch. The bound ones wait. The fourth will read what the third has found. The third will open what the first has sealed."*

The text is warm to the touch. As Character 3 watches, the script fades, leaving blank concrete. The room is now an ordinary janitor's closet.

**Scene 4 — The Aftermath**
The next day, Character 3 checks the recording of the transmission. The file is corrupt — static from 0230 to 0237. The dead frequency is silent again. There is no evidence of the transmission ever existing. But Character 3 remembers. They wrote down the coordinates. And they can't forget the phrase: "The third will open what the first has sealed."

**Recruitment**: Three weeks later, a woman calls the Shin Bet switchboard and asks for Character 3 by name. She identifies herself as "a consultant from the Ministry of Religious Affairs" — a cover Character 3's records confirm. Her actual name: Mrs. Grossman. Her message: *"I read your report on the 147.7 anomaly. There was no report. But I know you wrote one. I'd like to talk about the wall you found under the museum."*

**Handler Notes**:
- The transmission came from the Sicarii egregore. It's been waiting for someone with Character 3's specific skill set — someone who can read the mathematical language of the Pact.
- The phrase "the third will open what the first has sealed" refers to Character 3's role in the climax: they must open the deep vault at the Shrine of the Book.
- If this session is run after the main campaign has started, Character 3 has been tracking similar Aramaic transmissions on other dead frequencies. The signal is getting stronger.

### Character 4: "The Scroll That Knows Your Name" (Academic)

**Setting**: Qumran archives, Israel Museum, Jerusalem, 2025. Character 4 is a visiting scholar granted access to unclassified scroll fragments.

**Scene 1 — The Box**
Read or paraphrase:

> *"The archivist brings you a wooden box labeled 'Khim 4B — Unclassified, Inscribed Fragments, 1st Century CE.' The contents are tiny — scraps of parchment, most no bigger than your palm. 'Low historical value,' the archivist says. 'Mostly laundry lists and tax records. You can have the morning with them.' She leaves. You open the box. The smell hits you first — not dust and age, but salt water and myrrh. Fresh myrrh. As if these 2000-year-old fragments were prepared yesterday."*

Character 4 begins sorting. Most fragments are mundane. One is not.

**Scene 2 — The Fragment**
The fragment is 10cm x 15cm, the handwriting is elegant 1st-century Aramaic script. Character 4 begins to translate. The text reads:

> *"To the one from Gaza who reads this in the days of the great sealing's undoing: you were born in the city of the deep god, you fled to the land of the north, you returned to the land of your fathers carrying the languages of the dead. You are the fourth of the four who must choose. Do not be afraid. The Pact was written to be read. We who wrote it knew you would come. In the cave of echoes, in the hill of the whisper, you will find the next piece. Bring the fish. Bring the bronze. Bring the key that waits in the heart of the black wall."*

Character 4's hands are shaking. The fragment describes:
- Their birth in Gaza City
- Their scholarship to Canada ("the land of the north")
- Their return to Israel/Palestine as a scholar
- Their role as "the fourth of four"
- Specific instructions for the campaign

**Scene 3 — The Disappearance**
Character 4 reaches for their phone to photograph the fragment. The camera lens cracks — a hairline fracture, as if the glass was struck. They flag down the archivist. When they return to the table, the box is open. The fragment is gone. In its place: a fine, grey ash that smells of salt water. The other fragments are intact. The archivist has no explanation.

**Scene 4 — The Aftermath**
Character 4 tries to report the incident. The museum's security footage for that room shows Character 4 sitting at an empty table for 45 minutes, sorting fragments they appear to be pulling from an empty box. No fragment is visible on camera. But Character 4's hands are clearly shaking. The museum suggests they take a leave of absence.

**Recruitment**: A week later, Character 4 receives a letter with no return address. Inside: a photograph of the fragment — taken by someone else, in the same room, at the same time. On the back, written in fountain pen: *"We need to talk. I know where the cave of echoes is. — R. Grossman"*

**Handler Notes**:
- The fragment was left by the original Pact signatories. It's a Guide — a self-actualizing message that appears to the right reader at the right time.
- Character 4's role is unique: they are the only one who can read the War Scroll's original language without translation errors. A mistake in reading could trigger a Pact Level increase of +3.
- The fragment mentions three keys: "the fish" (Mariam's pendant / Dagon's mark), "the bronze" (an artifact in the Baal temple, Arc 2), "the key that waits in the heart of the black wall" (something in the Shrine of the Book's black basalt wall).
- If the player asks: yes, the fragment is still out there. Someone in the Order of the Broken Sica retrieved it from the box after Character 4 left. They know who Character 4 is.

## Arc 1: "The Silent Vigil" — Full Scenario

### Handler's Overview

**Location**: Khirbet Kafr Qaddum, east of Nablus, West Bank
**Date**: June 2026
**Duration**: One session (~3 hours)

**The mission**: A previously undocumented burial cave has been unsealed by a clash between local Palestinian youths and Israeli border police. Inside: 1st-century ossuaries containing fragmentary Aleppo Codex pages and the first evidence of the Binding Mark. The agents must infiltrate the closed military zone, retrieve the artifacts, and extract without causing an international incident.

**The obstacles**:
1. A Shin Bet lockdown — the area is sealed. The lead officer is competent and suspicious.
2. A settler group from Havat Yosef — armed, religiously motivated, claiming the cave for themselves.
3. A PIJ cell — wants the cave as a tactical firing position. Young, armed, scared.

**The cost**: Someone takes the blame for the theft. The agents choose who. The Group Bond takes the first hit.

**Pact Level setup**: Start at 3. The scenario includes opportunities for the agents to spend the Pact early, establishing the mechanic.

**Static Die setup**: Start at 1. The Shin Bet checkpoint is the first test.

---

### Scene 1: The Briefing — Mrs. Grossman's Call

**Setup**: The agents receive a secure message — a text, a dead-drop letter, or a call from a blocked number. Mrs. Grossman's voice is calm, measured, and slightly tired.

> *"I'm sending you a news packet. Read it carefully. There's a cave near Nablus that opened up a few days ago. The IDF has it locked down. The official story is security operations. The unofficial story is that something in that cave is very old and very important. I need you to get in, document what's inside, and bring me anything that looks like it doesn't belong in a 1st-century tomb.*
>
> *You have diplomatic cover — US Embassy cultural heritage liaison. You'll find your credentials in the usual drop. Your window is tonight, 2200-0400. The checkpoint guards rotate at 2300. The new shift is hungry and tired. Use that.*
>
> *One more thing: you're not the only people interested in this cave. There's a settler group that's been petitioning for access. And local intelligence suggests a militant cell has been scouting the area. Don't get caught between them.*
>
> *I'll be monitoring your progress. Don't make me come get you.*
>
> *— Grossman"*

The Handler then displays the Google News briefing (Session 1 tabs, already written above). Allow the players to study the tabs, ask questions, and plan their approach.

**Player Agency**: The agents choose how to approach — night infiltration, diplomatic bluff, diversion, or a combination. Each approach determines which obstacle they face first.

**Mechanical trigger**: The briefing establishes the Static Die. Place the d10 on the table showing 1.

---

### Scene 2: The Approach — Road to Nablus

**Setting**: Route 60, south of Nablus. Night. The landscape is rocky hills, olive terraces, the occasional village light in the distance. A military checkpoint blocks the road to the cave site.

**The Obstacle — Shin Bet Checkpoint**:

The checkpoint consists of: a reinforced position (concrete barriers, sandbags), two IDF soldiers (young, alert), one Shin Bet officer (civilian clothes, older, observant), a makeshift gate, floodlights.

The Shin Bet officer: **Rami Shoval**, 45, former undercover operative in the territories. He's been doing this job for 20 years. He's not corrupt, not lazy, and not easily fooled. He knows something is in that cave — not the supernatural truth, but he knows the antiquities team was excited before he locked them out. He's curious.

**Approach options**:

| Approach | Roll | Success | Failure |
|----------|------|---------|---------|
| **Diplomatic** (show embassy badge, claim UN cultural heritage inspection) | Cover or Bureaucracy | Pass through. Shoval notes their faces. | Shoval asks for paperwork they don't have. Detained for 30 min. Static +1. |
| **Stealth** (bypass the checkpoint, cross the hills on foot) | Stealth or Navigate | Avoid the checkpoint. Arrive at cave from the ridge. | Lost on the hills. Arrive 45 min late. Static +1. |
| **Diversion** (create a disturbance elsewhere, draw the guards away) | Demolitions or Persuade | Guards respond. 15 min window to pass. | Disturbance attracts more attention. Static +2. |
| **Bribery** | Persuade or Criminology | Shoval takes the money but remembers their faces. | Shoval arrests them for attempted bribery. Static +3. |

**All approaches**: Even on success, Shoval notes the agents' faces. He reports the encounter. This comes up in Session 2's Cover Story tab.

**If detained**: The agents can talk their way out (a second Bureaucracy or Fast Talk roll), but they lose time. The cave is now more dangerous — the settlers or PIJ may arrive while they're inside.

---

### Scene 3: The Cave

**Setting**: A 20-minute walk from the checkpoint, up a dry streambed, into the hillside. The cave entrance is a low arch, partially blocked by a fallen stone. The stone has been moved recently — fresh scrape marks on the ground.

**Cave Layout**:

```
                    ┌─────────────────┐
                    │   ENTRANCE      │  ← low arch, partially blocked
                    └────────┬────────┘
                             │
                    ┌────────┴────────┐
                    │  OUTER CHAMBER  │  ← 6m x 4m. Benches along walls.
                    │                 │    Oil lamp niches. Dust. Silence.
                    └────────┬────────┘
                             │
                    ┌────────┴────────┐
                    │  INNER CHAMBER  │  ← 4m x 4m. Three kokhim (niches)
                    │  [kok] [kok]    │    per wall. Each holds an ossuary.
                    │       [kok]     │    The air is cold. Colder than outside.
                    └────────┬────────┘
                             │
                    ┌────────┴────────┐
                    │  HIDDEN VAULT   │  ← Beneath a stone floor slab.
                    │                 │    The slab has the Binding Mark
                    │                 │    carved into its underside.
                    └─────────────────┘
```

**Describing the cave** (read or paraphrase):

> *"The entrance opens into a chamber cut from the living rock. The walls are tool-marked — chisel strokes from two thousand years ago. Benches line three walls, carved from the same stone. The air is dry, cold, still. Your footsteps echo once and then are swallowed.*
>
> *Three niches open in the far wall — kokhim, narrow burial shafts just wide enough for a body. Each contains a limestone ossuary, its lid in place. The ossuaries are carved with rosettes, the standard decoration for 1st-century Jewish burials. But one of them — the center ossuary — the rosette has been... altered. Recarved into something else. A star. A fish inside it.*
>
> *The floor of the chamber is uneven. Near the back wall, a rectangular slab sits slightly higher than the surrounding stone. You wouldn't notice it unless you were looking for it. Carved into the edge of the slab, just visible: the same star-and-fish symbol."*

**The Ossuaries**: Three ossuaries in the kokhim. Two are ordinary — contain bone fragments, typical for the period. The center ossuary is different:

- The lid is not sealed. It was opened recently — the person who recarved the rosette into the Binding Mark was here before the IDF lockdown.
- Inside: no bones. Instead, a bundle of linen cloth. Unwrapping it reveals the six Aleppo Codex fragments, wrapped in separate layers, each fragment preserved with cedar oil.
- The bottom of the ossuary is damp. Salt water. The smell is faint but unmistakable — the sea.

**The Hidden Vault**: Moving the floor slab requires a successful STR×5 roll (two agents can combine STR). Beneath: a narrow shaft, 2m deep, descending into a small chamber (2m x 2m).

Inside the hidden vault:
- The walls are covered in a single continuous inscription — Aramaic, 1st century. It's the opening lines of the Pact. A warning.
- On the floor: a bronze oil lamp, still containing oil. The wick is dry.
- The air is warmer here. Not hot — but warmer than the chamber above. As if something below is generating heat.

**The Pact's first contact**: When an agent touches the inscription on the wall, they feel a vibration — a low hum, barely perceptible. The letters seem to shift, rearranging themselves into a different arrangement. The agent who touches it sees, for a split second, a vision:
- A desert landscape at sunset
- Figures in white linen, standing in a circle
- A child being led to an altar
- The Binding Mark carved into the altar's base
Then the vision is gone. The agent takes 0/1 SAN (no loss on success, 1 on failure — the image is too fast to fully process).

**Mechanical trigger**: This is the campaign's first unnatural encounter. Ask for the SAN roll. Establish the tone: this is not a monster jumping out — it's reality slipping.

### Scene 4: The Pressure

The agents have a limited time in the cave. Two complications arrive, their timing determined by the agents' checkpoint approach:

| Checkpoint result | Time in cave before interruption |
|-------------------|----------------------------------|
| Success (any approach) | 60 minutes before the first arrival |
| Detained or delayed | 30 minutes before the first arrival |

**Arrival 1 — The Settlers** (30-60 min after agents enter):

A group of 4 men from the Havat Yosef settlement outpost. They've been monitoring the checkpoint and saw the agents pass. They've come to investigate.

**Leader**: **Itamar Cohen**, 38, former paratrooper, now a religious activist with a personal interest in the cave. He carries a pistol (openly), a flashlight, and a worn copy of the Book of Maccabees with handwritten notes in the margins.

Itamar believes the cave is the burial site of a Maccabean-era priest who hid scrolls "that would change the understanding of the Temple." He's half-right — there are scrolls (the Codex fragments) but they're not about the Temple.

**Approach to Itamar**:

| Approach | Roll | Outcome |
|----------|------|---------|
| **Cooperate** (show him a fragment, convince him it's biblical but not useful to him) | Occult or Persuade | Itamar leaves, intrigued. He may become a contact later. |
| **Deceive** (claim you're from the IAA and the artifacts are already catalogued) | Bureaucracy or Fast Talk | Itamar is skeptical. He calls the checkpoint to verify. If Shoval was suspicious, the lie fails. |
| **Intimidate** (flash the embassy badge, threaten diplomatic incident) | Cover or Firearms | Itamar backs down but remembers their faces. File under "future complications." |
| **Fight** | Combat | Loud. Shoval hears shots. IDF responds. Extraction becomes hostage situation. **Static +4. Campaign derailment risk.** |

**Arrival 2 — The PIJ Cell** (45-75 min after agents enter, or triggered by gunfire):

Two young men, ages 19 and 22, from the Al-Aqsa Martyrs' Brigades (PIJ affiliate). They've been watching the cave from a ridge 200m away. They saw the settlers arrive, heard voices, and came to investigate.

**Leader**: **Tariq**, 22, the older of the two. He's nervous, holding a vintage M16 that he doesn't know how to use properly. The younger one, **Rami** (not to be confused with the Shin Bet officer), is 19 and clearly terrified.

They don't know about the artifacts. They want the cave as a firing position for attacks on the settlement road below.

**Approach to Tariq**:

| Approach | Roll | Outcome |
|----------|------|---------|
| **Redirect** (convince him the cave is bad for his mission — too exposed, no escape route) | Tactics or Persuade | Tariq agrees. He leaves with Rami. They may be encountered again in the campaign. |
| **Bargain** (offer him something — money, information, a better position) | Persuade or Criminology | Tariq takes the offer but becomes suspicious of who the agents really are. |
| **Deceive** (claim to be fellow fighters, or international journalists sympathetic to the cause) | Fast Talk or Cover | Tariq buys it for now. But if the agents' cover story doesn't match what he knows, Static +2. |
| **Fight** | Combat | One of the young men dies. The other escapes. **Pact +1 (Blood Engine fed). Static +3. Character 2 (Medic) takes SAN loss.** |

**The Triangle**: If both settlers and PIJ arrive simultaneously, the cave becomes a three-way standoff. The agents must navigate between armed religious activists and armed militants while Shin Bet is on the way. This is the campaign's first test of the agents' ability to solve problems without violence.

**Handler note**: The fight option should feel like a real choice, but one with consequences. If the agents kill Tariq or Itamar, they've just fed the Blood Engine. The Pact ticks up. The game is telling them: violence is sometimes necessary, but it has a cosmic cost.

### Scene 5: The Cost

The agents escape the cave (or extract themselves from the standoff). They have the artifacts. They reach the extraction point — a rendezvous with a civilian vehicle on the Route 60 bypass.

But they left evidence. Or they were seen. Or their cover is blown.

**The newspaper the next morning** (Session 2's Cover Story):

Read or hand out:

> *"SHIN BET ARRESTS THREE LOCAL YOUTHS IN NABLUS ANTIQUITIES THEFT"*
>
> *Overnight, Shin Bet arrested three Palestinian teenagers in connection with a break-in at a restricted archaeological site near Nablus. The teenagers, residents of the nearby village of Kafr Qaddum, were found in possession of what authorities describe as "ancient artifacts of significant value." They face charges of vandalism, theft, and illegal excavation.*
>
> *A Shin Bet spokesperson stated that the individuals had been under surveillance for "suspected antiquities trafficking" and were apprehended while attempting to sell the artifacts on the black market. The artifacts have been transferred to the Israel Museum for evaluation.*

The agents know the truth: those teenagers are innocent. They were in the wrong place at the wrong time. The agents' theft was blamed on them.

**The Group Bond test**: Each agent rolls SAN (0/1d4). The knowledge that their operation cost innocent people their freedom is a direct hit. The player may:
- Accept the SAN loss (their character feels guilt)
- Project the trauma onto the Group Bond (the character argues that "it was necessary" — causing friction with another agent)

**Who takes the blame**: The Handler can vary this based on the agents' choices:
- If they fought the settlers: Itamar gives a description. Shin Bet blames the PIJ.
- If they fought the PIJ: Tariq's body is found. The teens are blamed as accomplices.
- If they avoided all conflict: The teens from the village are blamed simply for being nearby.
- If they deliberately framed one group: The agents can choose who takes the fall. But this requires a successful Cover or Criminology roll and carries its own SAN cost.

**The letter from Mrs. Grossman** (delivered the next day):

> *"Good work on the extraction. The artifacts are secure. But I read the news this morning. I know that's not what you wanted.*
>
> *This is the job. Sometimes the only way to save the world is to let the innocent take the hit. You learn to carry it or you break.*
>
> *I've analyzed the fragments. They're part of something larger — a map. There's a location in Syria that matches the coordinates. I'll have more for you soon.*
>
> *Rest. You've earned it. But don't rest too long.*
>
> *— Grossman"*

### Scene 6: Debrief — New Knowledge

After the session, the agents have:
1. The **Aleppo Codex fragments** (6 pieces, containing the seal map and one Pact Law each)
2. The **Binding Mark** (carved on the ossuary and the floor slab — they can reproduce it)
3. **Evidence of the Pact** (the inscription in the hidden vault, the vision)
4. A **witness account** (if they spoke to Itamar or Tariq, they have partial information about the region's supernatural history)

**Pact Level change**: +1 (end of Arc 1 scripted event — the agents disturbed the first seal, even if they were careful).

**Static Die**: Resets to 1 after the extraction (they're in the clear for now).

**Next mission hook**: Mrs. Grossman's letter mentions Syria. The coordinates from the Codex fragments point to a location on the Golan border — the Baal temple.

**The Handler reveals the Pact Track**: Show the players the reference card. Mark the Pact Level at 4 (started at 3, +1 for the arc). Explain: "This number will go up and down based on your choices. You can also spend it for power — ask me how."

Each session opens with four browser tabs the Handler displays or hands out. Layout guidelines:

**Tab 1 — World News** (Top-left)
- Looks like a real news site (Haaretz, Reuters, Al Jazeera)
- 2-3 headlines with short blurbs
- Real geopolitical context, no overt supernatural content
- Sets the mood: "this is a real place with real stakes"

**Tab 2 — Local Friction** (Top-right)
- Looks like a local news site or security report
- 1-2 detailed articles about the area the agents will operate in
- Contains useful intel: checkpoints, faction movements, civilian displacement
- The obstacles the agents must navigate, hidden in plain language

**Tab 3 — Cover Story** (Bottom-left)
- For Session 1: a Delta Green operational order disguised as an official document (embassy cable, security advisory, diplomatic note)
- For later sessions: how the media reported the agents' last mission — this changes based on their actions
- A reminder that actions have consequences

**Tab 4 — The Static** (Bottom-right)
- Looks like a fringe news site, Reddit post, WhatsApp screengrab, or leaked social media video
- The buried anomalous detail
- Short, ambiguous, unsettling
- Often dismissed as hoax or combat stress in-universe

**Suggested presentation**: A single A4 page with four panels, or a browser window screenshot with four tabs open. The Handler can also project it on a screen and reveal each tab in order.

## Prop Production Guide

### 1. US Embassy Cover Badge (Session 1)

**Purpose**: Cover identity prop. Each agent carries one. Creates a moment of "showing the badge" at checkpoints.

**Materials**:
- PVC or plastic card blanks (85.6mm x 54mm) — available from office supply stores, or cut from an old credit card
- Printable sticker paper
- Laminating pouch
- Lanyard with clip (optional but recommended)

**Steps**:
1. Create the badge front in any image editor: a US Embassy Tel Aviv seal, a photo placeholder, the agent's cover name, "REGIONAL SECURITY OFFICE" in small text, an expiration date 6 months in the future
2. Create the badge back: a barcode, a fake ID number starting with "EMB-TLV-", a line for "ISSUING AUTHORITY: RSO Tel Aviv"
3. Print on sticker paper, affix to card blank
4. Laminate. Trim edges.
5. Optional: scuff the edges with fine sandpaper to look worn

**Digital template**: Search for "US Embassy ID card template" for layout reference. Replace with your own text and a generic government seal.

**Table use**: When an agent shows the badge, they make a Cover or Bureaucracy roll. Success = waved through. Failure = the guard asks to see their "accompanying documentation" (which they may or may not have).

### 2. Mrs. Grossman's Recruitment Letters (1-on-1 sessions)

**Purpose**: The letter each character receives after their 1-on-1 session. Personal, mysterious, the first contact with Delta Green.

**Materials**:
- Off-white or cream A4 paper (not bright white — aged look)
- Fountain pen or fine-liner (black or dark blue ink)
- An envelope with no return address
- Optional: a single brass fish pendant (see prop #4) enclosed

**Text template** (customize per character):

```
[Character Name],

You don't know me, but I know about [the voice in the static / the
patient who dissolved / the transmission on 147.7 / the fragment that
spoke your name].

You are not the first person this has happened to. You will not be
the last. But you are the one I need now.

There are others like you. People who have seen what the world
tries to hide. I've been watching you for some time. You have the
skills we need. More importantly, you have the questions.

I can't tell you everything in a letter. But I can tell you this:
the war is not what it seems. The dead are not always gone. And
something under the land is waking up.

If you want answers — real answers — come to the Tmol Shilshom
café in Jerusalem, one week from today, at 14:00. Sit at the
table by the window. Order tea. I'll find you.

Come alone.

— R. Grossman
```

**Aging technique**:
1. Brew strong black tea. Let it cool.
2. Lightly brush the tea over the paper with a sponge. Blot dry with a paper towel.
3. Bake at 80°C (175°F) for 5 minutes, or let air dry.
4. Crumple the paper gently, then flatten it.
5. Write the letter on the aged paper. The ink will bleed slightly into the fibers — this looks correct.

### 3. Ossuary Photo / Binding Mark Diagram

**Purpose**: A visual reference for the Binding Mark symbol. Can be handed to players when they enter the Nablus cave.

**Materials**:
- Matte photo paper (4x6 inch)
- Printer
- Optional: a thin layer of clear drying glue mixed with a drop of brown paint, brushed over the photo to simulate dirt

**Design**:
The image shows a stone ossuary (rectangular, limestone, typical of 1st-century Judean burials). On its side, carved into the stone, is the Binding Mark. The photo should be slightly out of focus, dimly lit — as if taken by a phone in a dark cave.

**Binding Mark construction** (for the carving):
```
       \     /
        \   /
         \ /
    ──────█──────
         / \
        /   \
       /     \
         ┌───┐
         │ Ͻ │
         └───┘
```
The fish in the center should be stylized: a curved body with an open mouth showing small teeth, scales as chevrons, a forked tail. NOT the Christian ichthys symbol — this is older, predator-shaped.

**Free alternative**: Find a photo of a real 1st-century Jewish ossuary (Wikimedia Commons has many). Edit the image to add the Binding Mark over any existing rosette pattern. Print.

**Table use**: When players first see the photo, ask for an Occult or Archaeology roll to identify the symbol. On success: "This is a binding mark. It predates the Christian ichthys by centuries. It's Philistine — Dagon's symbol." On failure: "It looks like a star and a fish. You've never seen this exact arrangement before."

### 4. Brass Fish Pendant

**Purpose**: A tactile artifact the agents can physically hold. Used by Character 1 and Mariam.

**Materials**:
- Polymer clay (bronze or gold color, or paint after baking)
- A small jump ring (for the suspension loop)
- A leather cord or thin chain (to wear it)

**Steps**:
1. Shape the clay into a flat fish shape, ~5cm long. Leave a small hole at the top for the jump ring.
2. Use a toothpick or needle tool to incise scales (chevron patterns) and an eye.
3. Optional: press a small texture into the clay (a button, a piece of lace) to give it an ancient-cast look.
4. Bake per clay instructions.
5. Paint with bronze or verdigris acrylic paint. Wipe excess off with a paper towel to leave paint in the crevices — this simulates age.
6. Attach the jump ring and cord.

**No-clay alternative**: Buy a small brass fish charm from a craft store. File the back flat. Use a needle to incise the Binding Mark on the back. Age it by soaking in vinegar and salt for 2 hours, then rinsing.

**Table use**: Hand the physical pendant to the player whose character carries it. They can touch it, hold it, turn it over. The physical weight changes how they interact with the artifact — it's no longer just a line on a character sheet.

### 6. The "Static Die" Physical Die

**Purpose**: A large central d10 that everyone can see. When it hits 10, the Handler picks it up and rolls the complication.

**Material**: A single d10, larger than the rest of the dice at the table. Ideally:
- Opaque black with white numbers (ominous)
- Or a translucent "glow" color that looks unnatural
- At least 16mm, ideally 25mm (a "jumbo" die)

**Setup**: Place the die in the center of the table, showing 1. As the session progresses, the Handler rotates it to show the current value. No one needs to ask "what's the Static at?" — it's always visible.

**On 10**: The Handler picks up the die, announces "The heat crystallizes," rolls it behind the screen, and describes the complication. Then rotates the die back to 5.

### 7. The Pact Track (Table Display)

**Purpose**: A physical display of the Pact Level that everyone can see change.

**Option A — Glass beads**:
- A strip of adhesive felt or a wooden stand with 11 slots (or a drawn line on paper)
- 10 beads: 2 green, 3 yellow, 3 red, 2 black
- As the Pact Level changes, move the beads
- The sound of a bead clicking into place is an audio cue

**Option B — Playing cards**:
- A row of 10 facedown cards. Flip one face-up for each Pact Level.
- The face-up cards reveal increasingly disturbing images (choose cards that match — skeletons, towers, the moon)
- The players don't know what the last card shows until they flip it

**Option C — Digital counter**:
- A phone or tablet at the table showing a number 0-10
- Use a free counter app, or just a Notes document
- Changes with a deliberate swipe or tap

**Table use**: Whenever the Pact Level changes, say "The Pact ticks up/down by X." Reach over and move the bead / flip the card / tap the screen. Let the silence hang for a moment. Then continue.

### 8. Aleppo Codex Fragments (Arc 1 Artifact)

**Purpose**: A physical representation of the codex pages the agents find in the Nablus cave. They can touch them, try to read them, and worry about damaging them.

**Materials**:
- Unbleached parchment-style paper (or thick brown craft paper, cut to ~10cm x 15cm)
- Black tea for aging (see prop #2)
- A calligraphy pen or thin marker
- A lighter (for burn marks)

**Steps**:
1. Age 6 sheets of paper using the tea method
2. Write Hebrew-style text on each sheet. It doesn't need to be real Hebrew — just simulated letter shapes that look authentic. Leave gaps for where the "real" text would go.
3. Write the actual campaign clues in UV-visible ink or very faint pencil between the lines. The players have to look closely to find them.
4. Burn one corner of each sheet slightly with a lighter. Blow out immediately. The charred edge makes it feel ancient and fragile.
5. Store the fragments in a box or envelope labeled "KHIRBET 4B — UNCLASSIFIED"

**UV ink alternative**: Write the geographic annotations (seal coordinates) between the lines using a UV pen. Players must think to use a blacklight or UV flashlight. Most phone cameras can detect UV ink as a faint purple glow. If they don't use one, they miss the clues.

**Table use**: When the agents find the fragments, hand the physical papers to the players. Let them handle them. Warn them (jokingly or seriously) not to tear them. The fragility is part of the experience.

### 9. Mrs. Grossman's Glasses (Ambient Prop)

**Purpose**: A prop for the Handler, not the players. Wearing a pair of glasses similar to Mrs. Grossman's when you're in character as her helps differentiate Handler-voice from Mrs. Grossman-voice.

**Materials**: An inexpensive pair of reading glasses from a drugstore. Attach a beaded eyeglass band / chain to the temples (available at any pharmacy).

**Table use**: Put the glasses on when you speak as Mrs. Grossman. Take them off when you're narrating as the Handler. This simple physical cue signals to players "this is an NPC talking, not the narrator."

## Music & Sound Guide

### Philosophy

This campaign lives in the contrast between ancient and modern. The music should do the same: middle-eastern classical instrumentation (oud, kamancheh, ney, darbuka) colliding with industrial drone, ambient texture, and electronic grit. No orchestral fantasy scores. No heroic anthems. The music should feel like the land itself is humming.

### Practical Playback

**Option A — Single playlist** (simplest):
Create one campaign playlist with all ambient tracks. Shuffle at low volume. Manually queue scene-specific tracks.

**Option B — Scene-queued** (best):
Create separate playlists or use a tool like Syrinscape / Tabletop Audio:

```
Playlist 1: "Campaign Ambient" — always-on background drone
Playlist 2: "Briefing" — Google News opener sounds
Playlist 3: "Combat / Tension" — high-energy tracks for chases and firefights
Playlist 4: "Unnatural" — cosmic horror tracks for egregore encounters
```

**Volume rule**: Music should be at 20-30% of speaking volume during dialogue, 10% during description, 40-50% during action scenes with no talking. If players have to raise their voices, the music is too loud.

**The silence rule**: After a major revelation or SAN loss, cut the music entirely. Let the silence last 10-15 seconds before describing the next moment. The absence of sound is more disturbing than any track.

## One-Shot: "The Golan Breach" (Mrs. Grossman's Origin)

### Overview

**Setting**: Golan Heights, October 1973. The Yom Kippur War. Day 6.
**Duration**: One session (~2-3 hours)
**Players**: 4. Each plays a member of Rivka Grossman's original team. The Handler already knows the ending — only Rivka survives. The players do not know this.

**Why run this**: This one-shot achieves three things:
1. It lets players experience the campaign's foundational event
2. It reveals Mrs. Grossman's backstory through play, not exposition
3. It creates a tragic emotional anchor — when Rivka appears in the main campaign, the players remember what she survived

**Canon rule**: The events of this one-shot are fixed. Rivka touches the inscription, receives the vision, and survives. The others break. Player choices determine *how* they break and *what Rivka carries forward* from each death.

### Characters

#### Rivka Grossman (25) — The Archaeologist

Played by a player. (The Handler should brief this player afterward: "You now know what Rivka knows. You can use this information to inform your choices in the main campaign — but remember, Rivka has never told anyone the full truth of what she saw.")

| Stat | Value |
|------|-------|
| **STR** 10 | **CON** 12 | **DEX** 13 | **INT** 16 | **POW** 15 | **CHA** 13 |
| **HP** 11 | **WP** 15 | **SAN** 70 |

**Skills**: Archaeology 70%, History 60%, Occult 30%, Ancient Languages (Hebrew, Aramaic, Ugaritic) 60%, Persuade 40%, Navigate 40%, First Aid 30%

**Equipment**: Field notebook, measuring tape, camera (film), flashlight, canteen, a worn copy of *Canaanite Religion* by W.F. Albright.

**Notes**: Rivka is young, brilliant, and secular. She believes the ancient texts are historical documents, not living truths. This session will prove her wrong. The player should feel her certainty crumbling.

He volunteered for this antiquities detail because he wanted to be anywhere other than the front. ### Session Structure

#### Scene 1: The War (15 min)

**Setup**: The team is huddled in a half-destroyed building near the Golan front. Syrian shelling is intermittent. The ground shakes every few minutes.

Read or paraphrase:

> *"October 12th, 1973. Six days since the war started. You've been attached to a salvage unit — your job is to document archaeological sites before the fighting destroys them entirely. It's a cover, mostly. The real mission is to make sure Syrian intelligence hasn't been using ancient sites to cache weapons. But at 1400 hours, a shell hit a hillside two kilometers east of here. The forward observation post reports that the collapse exposed a buried structure. Stonework. Not natural. Your orders: secure the site, assess the structure, report back."*

**Small scene**: Before they move out, each character has a moment:
- Rivka receives a field radio message from her university advisor: *"Be careful. There are things in the ground older than any text."* She dismisses it.
- Amos tries to raise his brother's battalion on the radio. Static.
- Nadia notices the shelling pattern: the Syrian guns are hitting specific coordinates in a grid pattern. It looks like they're trying to uncover something, not destroy it.
- Eli checks his charges, makes sure everyone has water. He's done this before. He knows the difference between a combat mission and a death march.

#### Scene 2: The Hillside (20 min)

The team drives to the site in a half-track. The landscape is moonscape — shell craters, burned vehicles, the occasional tree stripped of its leaves by blast waves.

The structure: a basalt block wall, partially exposed by the collapse. The entrance is a low arch, 1.5m high, choked with rubble. Eli clears it with a controlled charge (Demolitions roll — on failure, the blast is louder than intended, attracting attention).

Inside: a descending corridor, 20m long, cut from the living rock. The walls are smooth — not toolmarked like Iron Age construction, but smooth as if the stone was *melted* and resolidified.

**The smell**: Salt water. Myrrh. Burned oil. The same smell that will define the campaign.

**Environmental detail**: The corridor is not straight. It curves slightly, always to the left. A compass reading shows deviation — the corridor is spiraling, but the team hasn't turned enough to justify the displacement.

**Mechanical trigger**: Nadia notices that the stone is warm. Not surface-warm from the sun. Warm from within. Rivka takes a sample for later analysis.

#### Scene 3: The Chamber (30 min)

The corridor opens into a circular chamber, 10m in diameter. The ceiling is a perfect dome. No visible supports.

At the center: a stone plinth. On the plinth: a bronze-and-clay jar, sealed with lead and wax. The stasis jar.

The walls: covered in a continuous Aramaic inscription, arranged in concentric rings that spiral from the floor to the dome's apex.

**Nadia reads the inscription**: It begins with a formula she recognizes from later Jewish mystical texts — but this version is older, purer, and slightly different. It's not addressed to God. It's addressed to *them* — the entities, by name:

> *"To Ba'al-Hammon, who burns the fields: you are bound here. To Dagon, who fills the deep: you are bound here. To the spirit of the knife, who ends the line: you are bound here. By the word spoken in the cave of seven echoes, by the blood of the covenant, by the witness of the earth and sky: you are bound. Until the third generation after the return, until the sons of the sons forget the price. Then you may speak again."*

**Handler note**: This is the first time the players hear the full Pact formula. It should feel ancient, terrifying, and *read* — like a legal document, not a prayer.

**The stasis jar**: Eli opens it (requires a STR×5 or a careful Demolitions roll). Inside: a bronze shekel (the same artifact as Arc 2's Bronze Shekel of Ba'al), wrapped in linen. The linen is damp. Salt water.

When the jar is opened, the temperature in the chamber drops 10 degrees. The torchlight flickers. A low hum begins, coming from the walls.

#### Scene 4: The Voice (30 min)

Amos's backpack radio activates on its own. The voice is clear, calm, and speaking in Hebrew — but Hebrew that includes words that haven't been spoken since the 1st century:

> *"Amos ben Yehuda. Your brother is with us now. He died calling your name. Would you like to hear it?"*

The radio plays a recording of Amos's brother's last moments. It is accurate. Amos knows it's accurate. He starts screaming at the radio, demanding it stop.

**Nadia** begins translating faster, trying to finish the inscription before — she doesn't know what. The words start shifting as she reads them, rearranging themselves into a response. The walls are *talking back*.

**Eli** stands at the entrance, watching the corridor. He reports that the light at the end of the corridor is fading — not like sunset, but like something is absorbing the light. He draws his sidearm.

**Rivka** feels the pull. The inscription is not just text — it's a trap. A snare for attention. The more you read, the more it wants you to touch it.

**The choice**: Each character is offered something by the voice in the walls:
- **Amos**: "Your brother can come back. You just have to let us use your voice to call him."
- **Nadia**: "You've spent your life translating dead languages. We can teach you the language that *creates*. Touch the wall."
- **Eli**: "You've always known the world is stranger than they tell you. Stop pretending. Look."
- **Rivka**: "You're the one we've been waiting for. The archaeologist who digs too deep. The one who finds what should stay buried. Touch the wall. See what we are."

**Resolution**: The players choose how their characters respond. The outcomes are fixed — only Rivka survives — but how it happens is player-driven:
- Amos may shoot the radio. He may listen to his brother die again. He may try to make the bargain.
- Nadia may complete the translation. She may refuse. She may touch the wall.
- Eli may pull the team out. He may stand guard. He may walk into the darkness to see what's there.

**The breaking** (happens after the vision, regardless of choices):
When Rivka touches the inscription, she receives the 47-second vision. She sees:
- The original Pact signing (three figures in white)
- The egregores as they are bound (not monsters — shapes of fire, water, and darkness)
- Flashes of the future: the 2026 war, the Shrine of the Book, four figures standing over a crystal rod, a woman with a beaded band (herself, older)
- A voice: *"You will carry this for 53 years. Then you will pass it to the four. Do not fail."*

When Rivka returns from the vision, the others are broken:
- **Nadia** is sitting against the wall, eyes open, whispering the Pact formula in a loop. She will never stop. She is committed to a psychiatric hospital in Jerusalem and dies in 1989 without speaking again.
- **Amos** shot himself during Rivka's vision — the voice in the radio offered him his brother and he refused the bargain, so the voice showed him every death his brother could have died instead. He put the pistol in his mouth.
- **Eli** is standing at the entrance, facing outward. His eyes are open. He is breathing. He is gone — his mind evacuated. His body lives for another 40 years in a state facility, never waking.

Only Rivka is intact. She does not know why.

#### Scene 5: The Debrief (20 min)

American military personnel arrive at the site. They secure the chamber. They take the shekel. They photograph the walls. They ask Rivka questions.

One man stands apart: **Thomas Keane**, 50, civilian clothes, steady eyes. He waits until the others are finished, then sits down across from Rivka at a field table.

> *"You survived because you listened. Everyone else tried to answer, to fight, to bargain, to resist. You just stood there and listened. That's rare. We need people like you."*

He opens a briefcase. Inside: a folder with PROJECTOV COVENANT stamped in red. He slides a photograph across the table — a black-and-white image of a symbol: the Binding Mark.

> *"This is the mark of the contract that binds them. You're going to see it everywhere, from now on. In stone. In water. In the shape of a wound. Trust me. You can't unsee it."*

Rivka looks at the photograph. The mark is carved into the altar in her vision. She knows it's real.

> *"What about my team?"*
>
> *"They're gone. Not dead — but gone. You're the only one left who saw what was in that room and can still talk about it.*
>
> *Welcome to the watch, Dr. Grossman. There's no retirement."*

### Epilogue: Handler Notes for the One-Shot

**Tone**: This session is a tragedy. The players should feel the weight of inevitability. Their choices matter for *how* their characters break, not whether. The horror is watching it happen and being unable to stop it.

**Connection to the main campaign**:
- What Rivka saw in the vision: the four 2026 agents, the Shrine of the Book, the War Scroll, the glass beads (the Pact Track). She's been preparing for this campaign for 53 years.
- The shekel recovered from the chamber is the same artifact the agents find in Arc 2. Project Covenant took it in 1973. How did it end up back in the Baal temple?
