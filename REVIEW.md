# REVIEW — The Levantine Engine (Campaign Review)

This is a short, constructive review of the Levantine Engine campaign: what works, what to watch for, and practical suggestions to tighten writing, mechanics, and table safety. I focused on tone, playability, mechanical clarity, and cultural sensitivity.

## Overview

This is an evocative, well-structured Delta Green campaign that ties local history, archaeology, and cosmic horror into an integrated trilemma (Moloch / Dagon / Sicarii). The campaign has strong set-pieces, clear pacing across arcs, and excellent physical props and sensory cues that will create memorable table moments.

## Strengths

- Strong central conceit: the Pact / Debt mechanic is elegant and narratively meaningful — players make weighty trade-offs rather than grinding resources.
- Excellent session flow and handouts (Google News tabs, Player Reference). This makes the table presentation smooth and cinematic.
- Rich NPCs with believable motivations and moral ambiguity (Rivka, Yael, Mariam, Salim) — players will care about choices.
- Clever use of artifacts and tangible props (pendant, fragments, Binding Mark). Physical objects will deepen immersion.
- The 1-on-1 origin sessions provide focused character hooks and make the team dynamics feel earned.

## Risks & Concerns

1. Real-world setting and active conflict
   - The campaign is explicitly set in Israel/Palestine/Syria and uses contemporary factions, real places, and current events. This raises risks of real-world offense, retraumatization, and political entanglement.
   - Players with lived experience could be triggered by depictions of violence, displacement, or medical trauma.

2. Moral framing and agency
   - The trilemma (no clean choice) is powerful but can implicitly suggest there are no peaceful options. Ensure players do not feel the game forces them into ethically abusive choices without meaningful alternatives.

3. Representation and stereotyping
   - Avoid flattening complex communities into single factions or villain tropes. Be careful with language about civilians, militias, religious groups, and avoid equating faith with extremism.

4. Mechanics complexity / ambiguity
   - Pact spending costs and Static Die interactions are clear in spirit, but some edge cases (timing of Static increments, exact costs for artifact use, WP economy) should be spelled out for the Handler.

## Practical Recommendations

1. Run a Session Zero and written safety brief
   - Include content warnings (violence, political conflict, medical trauma, torture, death of civilians) and ask for hard/soft limits.
   - Establish lines (never include X) and veils (fade-to-black rules) and a debrief/care plan after sessions with heavy content.

2. Sensitivity review and cultural consultation
   - If possible, get a sensitivity read from someone with relevant cultural knowledge (regional historian, local cultural consultant) to avoid inadvertent appropriation or harmful framing.

3. Provide neutral-language alternatives
   - Offer optional reskins for groups/locations (fictionalized names/places) for tables that prefer to avoid using real contemporary groups. The mechanics and themes work equally well with lightly fictionalized geography.

4. Clarify mechanics in a short FAQ/cheat-sheet
   - Examples: exact Pact cost for each artifact ability, how WP is recovered (if at all), sequence for Static die changes (when to apply during a scene), and how SAN / Group Bond interactions resolve mechanically.
   - Add sample play transcripts showing a Pact spend and ensuing Static increments to teach the table.

5. Make the 1-on-1s modular
   - Offer an optional table of tones for each one-on-one (grim, ambiguous, hopeful) so GMs can match player comfort. Provide a short “safe run” variant that reduces graphic detail.

6. Tone & language guidance for the Handler
   - Include recommended phrasing for describing violence without sensationalizing (focus on consequence, not gore). Give example scripts for Mrs. Grossman and for how to read Tab 4 (The Static) without endorsing a political stance.

7. Artifact handling and permanence
   - Add explicit rules for how fragile artifacts (Aleppo fragments) degrade, how restoration attempts work, and what happens if an artifact is sold/seized. Make the cost/benefit clear so players make informed choices.

8. Faction ambiguity
   - Encourage the Handler to seed evidence that complicates neat moral labels (Project Gibeon has humane motives, Keepers have valid concerns). This preserves the campaign's ethical tension while avoiding caricature.

## Mechanical tweaks (small)

- Pact/Static timing: state whether Pact increases from events are applied immediately or at end of scene; do the same for Static. (Recommendation: immediate for Pact spends, end-of-scene roll for large events.)
- Define WP economy: how many WP do characters start with, how does it regenerate between sessions? Is spending WP per-session limited?
- Add a short table for common complications when Static hits 10 tied to each Arc (Nablus, Golan, Gaza, Jerusalem) so complications feel thematic.
- Group Bond resolution: give a step-by-step rule for projecting SAN into the Bond (who chooses, how to roleplay, immediate mechanical exchange).

## Table-ready safety handouts (proposed additions)

- One-page Session Zero template (content warnings, lines/veils, aftercare plan, consent check-in schedule).
- One-page Cultural Sensitivity Note for the Handler (phrasing tips, trigger list, optional fictionalization checklist).
- Quick mechanical FAQ (1 page) with examples of Pact spends and Static resolution.

## Props / Production suggestions

- The physical props are a major strength. Consider having printable alternate graphics that fictionalize locations (if GMs want distance from real-world politics).
- For the Aleppo fragments, include a clean “copy” set with the clues in plain text (for the Handler) and a “player” set that is partially obfuscated.
- Offer digital assets (4-tab briefing PNG, ossuary photo, pendant image) sized for print and for virtual tabletops.
