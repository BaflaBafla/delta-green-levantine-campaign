# THOUGHTS — Freeform Brainstorming

## The Pact / Debt System
- The original binding was a contract. The egregores agreed to be bound.
- Who signed for humanity? The First-Century syncretic sect. But maybe not *all* of them signed.
- What if the sect split: those who bound vs. those who collaborated? The Order of the Broken Sica might descend from the collaborators — keeping the pact alive, not just maintaining balance.
- Debt metaphor: The egregores lent psychic power to the land. The interest is human suffering. The principal is due.
- What does "paying off the debt" look like? A one-time massive sacrifice? A ritual that costs the caster's humanity? A choice to redirect the debt to something else?

## The Fourth Path
- What if the heroic choice isn't "destroy the egregores and accept reality" but rather "renegotiate the Pact on new terms?"
- The agents, as outsiders with dual identities, represent something the egregores haven't encountered: people who belong fully to neither side. This might let them rewrite the contract.
- Could the agents themselves become the new "watchers" — taking the place of the Order, binding themselves to the land?

## Mrs. Grossman

### Design Rationale

**The 1973 Golan Breach**: I chose the Yom Kippur War as her origin because it's the foundational trauma of modern Israeli identity — a surprise attack, existential threat, and the discovery that the old gods are real. It also links directly to Arc 2 (the Baal temple is the same location). This creates a structural echo: the campaign opens with Rivka sending agents to the same hill where she was broken 53 years ago.

**The ALS / Pact debt**: She's dying of something that looks natural (ALS) but is actually supernatural (Pact interest). This is classic Delta Green — the conspiracy doesn't end with a gunshot, it ends with a doctor's visit and a terminal diagnosis. The mundane horror of a degenerative disease is more可怕 than any monster.

**The glasses as a visual cue**: The amber-tinted lenses + beaded band are distinctive enough that players will recognize them across a room. When the Handler puts them on (as a prop), the players know it's Rivka speaking. When/if she gives them away, the players know what it means — she's not coming back.

**Why no first name**: "Mrs. Grossman" creates professional distance. She's not their friend, she's their Handler. The agents should feel like they know her by the end of the campaign — but realize they never learned her first name.

**The wedding band on the right hand**: In Jewish tradition, the wedding ring is placed on the right index finger, not the left ring finger. Rivka's right-hand wedding band is a quiet cultural detail that Israeli players will recognize and international players will wonder about. Her husband died in 1991 — the same year Thomas Keane died. She never remarried. She never took the ring off.

### Unanswered Questions for Future Development

- What was Rivka's original name? She abandoned it when she joined Project Covenant. Is there a file somewhere with her real identity?
- Who does she report to now? Project Covenant is disbanded, and Delta Green's Program doesn't officially operate in Israel. Is she truly independent, or is there someone above her?
- The wedding band — who was her husband? Was he Thomas Keane? (Plausible, though problematic — her training officer and lover.) Or was it someone before the war, someone she lost in 1973?
- If she survives the campaign, where does she go? She's been in Israel for 68 years. She's never lived anywhere else.

## The 1-on-1 Origin Sessions
- Each player gets a solo session before the campaign.
- These should establish their *personal* connection to the Unnatural — not just their backstory.
- Maybe each character has already encountered the egregore's influence and doesn't know it.
- The Tactical Lead (Character 1): During their IDF service, they were part of an operation that went wrong. Everyone else died. They survived because they heard a voice telling them where to stand. Dagon? Moloch? Or something else?
- The Medic (Character 2): In a Gaza clinic, they treated a patient who had no physical wounds but was *decaying from the inside*. The smell was ancient salt water. They've been researching ever since.
- The Intelligence Asset (Character 3): Intercepted a transmission that was pure Aramaic. Decrypted it: it was a recursive mathematical proof that proved the existence of God. Or something that wants to be God. They've been tracking similar signals ever since.
- The Academic (Character 4): In the Qumran archives, they found a scroll fragment that *mentions them by name*. Written 2000 years ago. The fragment describes their role in the coming renegotiation.

## Mechanics to Play With
- **The Pact Track**: A campaign-long counter that tracks how badly the Pact is being violated. Starts at 0 (stable) and goes to 10 (the egregores fully manifest). Certain actions increase it (seal damage, mass casualties, ritual violations). Certain actions decrease it (reparations, seal repairs, sacrifices).
- **Bond Projection with Consequences**: The Group Bond is a shared resource. Projecting onto it requires the player to describe how their character lashes out at a teammate. What if the receiving player can refuse to accept the projection — causing it to bounce back and hit harder?
- **Language as Sorcery**: Speaking ancient Hebrew/Aramaic/Ugaritic in the right place at the right time has real effects. The Academic's language skills become tactical assets. The players need to decide *what to say* — literally choose words.
- **The Static Die**: A campaign-wide d10 that starts at 1 and increases each time the agents do something unnatural. On a 10, something bad happens in the next scene. Resets when the agents do something to lower their profile.

## Pact System Design Notes

### Design Goals
- **Player-facing**: They see the numbers. They decide when to spend. No hidden tracks.
- **Moral weight**: Spending the Pact is the "easy button" but it pushes the world toward cosmic collapse. Every point spent is a choice.
- **Two pressures**: Cosmic (Pact) and mundane (Static). Players manage both. High Pact makes the Static problem worse — the egregores' influence draws the mundane world's attention.
- **No resource attrition**: Unlike SAN which erodes permanently, the Pact can go up AND down. This gives players a reversible resource to manage, which feels more like a strategy game and less like a death spiral.

### Why "Spend to Reroll" Is Important
Delta Green is lethal. A single failed Luck roll can end a character. Giving players a "I refuse to accept that outcome" button that *they choose to press* — and that has a clear, escalating cost — turns a random tragedy into a deliberate sacrifice. It makes the cosmic horror personal: "I could have let that bullet hit me. Instead, I made the world worse."

### Pact vs. Sanity
SAN and the Pact should pull in opposite directions:
- Using SAN: you protect your mind by projecting trauma onto Bonds (making the world worse personally)
- Using the Pact: you protect yourself by feeding the egregores (making the world worse cosmically)
- The heroic path: accept the SAN hit, don't touch the Pact. The pragmatic path: spend the Pact, preserve your SAN. The tension is the point.

### Pact Terms as Plot Devices
Each egregore has a Pact Term. These are the specific contract clauses the egregores want fulfilled. The agents can:
- Fulfill them (lowers the Pact Track — the egregores honour the deal)
- Violate them (raises the Pact Track — the egregores get angry)
- Renegotiate them (the Fourth Path — requires the War Scroll and a high-risk ritual)

What if the three Pact Terms interact? E.g., fulfilling Dagon's term ("What lies beneath") requires deepening the Gaza tunnels, which automatically fulfills one of Moloch's requirements ("First-born of every generation" — more tunnel collapses kill more young soldiers). The agents can't fulfill one without feeding another.

### Pact Track Visuals
On the table: a line of 10 glass beads or poker chips. Green at 0, yellow at 3-4, red at 7+, black at 10. Players physically move the beads. The sound of a bead clicking into red zone is its own audio cue.

### Static Die Visual
On the table: a large d10 that sits in the center. Players can see the current value. On 10, the Handler picks it up and rolls for the complication.

## NPC Design Notes

### Yael Talmor — The Reluctant Patriot
- She's the "hard woman making hard choices" archetype, but with the twist that she *hates* the choices she's making
- Her arc: starts as an obstacle, becomes a reluctant ally, then a damsel in distress (she needs saving from her own creation)
- The "nine casualty operation" — the next Moloch numerology pattern. If the Agents don't intervene, Yael will be forced to order an operation that kills exactly nine young soldiers. The number 9 is significant in Canaanite numerology — the number of months of gestation, completion, sacrifice.
- Her family: she has a daughter serving in the IDF. The daughter doesn't know what Yael does. This is her Bond.

### Mariam Abu Rashed — The Doctor Who Bargained
- Her mutation progression: webbed fingers → gills → scales → the final transformation
- Each campaign arc, she's a little less human. By Arc 3, she might not be able to leave the tunnels anymore.
- The brass fish pendant: it's a Pact artifact. It lets her communicate with Dagon without entering the deep. It's also tracking her — Dagon knows where she is at all times.
- Her redemption possibility: if the Agents can find a way to transfer the psychic sponge function to something else (a ritual site, a machine, themselves), Mariam would give anything to be free.

### Salim Jaber — The Ancient Custodian
- The "Last Vigil" knife: obsidian, Roman-era, the same type used by the original Sicarii. Salim has had it since his predecessor died in 1984. The ritual requires slitting one's own throat in a specific geographic pattern.
- His limp: in 1987, during the First Intifada, Salim assassinated a Palestinian militant who was about to trigger a massive Moloch-feeding operation. The militant's brother stabbed Salim in the hip before dying. Salim still visits the brother's grave every year.
- His arc: if the Agents convince him a Fourth Path exists, he will be their most powerful ally — he knows every seal location, every ritual, every cult member. But if they fail to convince him, he will trigger the Last Vigil before letting them break the Pact.
- The Order's membership: approximately 47 active watchers across Israel, Palestine, Syria, Lebanon, and Jordan. They communicate through church charity networks, Druze religious gatherings, and encrypted messages hidden in synagogue newsletters.

### Faction Dynamics
- Yael and Salim have met. Once. In 2019, at a dig site near Beit Shemesh. They recognized each other immediately. Neither spoke of it. Salim left a single white stone on the dashboard of Yael's car — the watcher's mark of "I see you, but I will not act today."
- Mariam doesn't know the Order exists. She has heard rumors of "the priests who watch" but dismisses them as folk tales.
- The three factions converge at the Shrine of the Book — but they don't arrive at the same time. A suggested arrival order:
  1. The Keepers (first to arrive, through a tunnel they've dug from the Old City aqueduct)
  2. Project Gibeon (second, arrived via official cover — "security inspection")
  3. The Order (last, arriving through a subterranean passage only they know, dating to the 1st century)
